# Naming a Generation: Regional and Gender Patterns in 21st-Century American Baby Naming

Statistics capstone project · Winona State University · Spring 2026
**Courtney Casey** · B.S. Statistics, minors in Data Science and Mathematics
Advisor: Dr. Tisha Hooks, Department of Mathematics and Statistics

📄 **[Read the full paper (PDF)](Naming%20A%20Generation.pdf)**

## Overview

Using U.S. Social Security baby name records from 2000 and 2024, this study measured how naming diversity and regional naming preferences changed across female and male births in the four U.S. Census regions (Northeast, Midwest, South, West).

## Key questions

- Do female and male names differ in overall naming diversity?
- How did naming diversity change between 2000 and 2024 across regions and sex?
- How do regional top-ten name rankings deviate from national patterns, and where is that deviation strongest?

## Methods

All analysis was performed in **R**.

- **Diversity Index** (inverse Simpson) computed from full name distributions to capture both variety and concentration of names
- **Rank deviation** between regional and national top-ten rankings to quantify regional departure from national trends
- **Kruskal–Wallis tests** by year and sex, with **pairwise Wilcoxon rank sum tests** (Benjamini–Hochberg adjustment) to locate specific regional differences
- **Chi-square tests** of regional top-ten name distributions, with **Cramér's V** for association strength
- Case study of the name *Hudson* tracing how regional adoption timing differed across the country

## Key findings

- Naming diversity increased substantially from 2000 to 2024 in every region, and female names were consistently more diverse than male names
- The South showed the highest naming diversity by 2024; the West and Midwest departed most consistently from national top-ten rankings
- Regional differences in 2024 female rank deviation were statistically significant, and regional top-ten distributions differed significantly across all year/sex comparisons
- The *Hudson* case study showed regional adoption is not uniform — Midwestern growth came earlier and faster than elsewhere
